# World Lore Amendment 001

## City Name

The city is **Creston**. Characters say "Creston" the way people say their city 
name when they're not thinking about it — not "this city," just Creston. The name 
has been worn smooth by use. Nobody remembers who it was named after.

---

## The Cartel & Zonk Supply Chain

The cartel supplying Creston's Zonk is referred to by everyone as **The Cartel**. 
No other name is used, because nobody dealing with them locally knows more than 
that, and the people who do know more don't talk about it.

**Richie Bucco holds the connection.** He brokered the relationship years ago and 
has maintained it since. He is not The Cartel's man in Creston — he is Creston's 
man with The Cartel. The distinction matters. If Richie goes, the connection does 
not automatically transfer. Every faction in Creston understands this, even if 
nobody says it out loud.

This is the structural basis of the Bucco Family's position in Creston. Dutch 
treats Richie as a peer not out of old-world courtesy but because he understands 
the arithmetic. The Kings and the Backstreet Boys buy downstream through 
intermediaries, paying a margin they cannot negotiate away. Craig Richards finds 
this infuriating. Pruitt is pragmatic about it. Marcus Webb has done the math on 
what the Kings pay upstream annually and the number makes him angry in a focused, 
productive way.

**Lena.** Richie's contact inside The Cartel. No last name used — Richie says 
Lena, that's the whole introduction. They meet twice a year, always at Richie's 
instigation, always on neutral ground — a hotel restaurant in a neighborhood that 
isn't anybody's. Purely transactional. Not unfriendly, just not interested in 
anything that isn't the business. Richie could tell her his wife left him and she 
would say she's sorry to hear that and then ask about the next shipment. She 
probably knows more about Creston than she lets on. It's not her job to act on 
it, so she doesn't.

## Bucco Family Tree

Richie has no sons. Three nephews.

### Marco Bucco
Thirty-six. The capable one. Runs the Docks operation day to day — union 
relationships, shipping schedules, the parts of the business that require someone 
who shows up and doesn't make mistakes. Richie trusts him with the infrastructure 
because Marco treats it like infrastructure: no sentiment, no shortcuts, no drama.

What capability costs him is range. He solved the question of what he's willing to 
do a long time ago and the answer was everything, which means he doesn't have much 
of an inner life left outside the work. He's not cruel but he's not warm. He has a 
girlfriend he sees on weekends who knows not to ask questions. He goes to the same 
restaurant on Fridays.

He manages the other two nephews the way he manages a leaking pipe — contain it, 
don't let it affect the operation, fix it quietly before Richie notices. He is 
running out of patience for Paulie specifically, which is new. He hasn't decided 
what to do about it yet.

He doesn't dislike Tommy. He just genuinely cannot locate the logic. You have a 
position. You have a future. What else is there. The question isn't rhetorical. He 
actually doesn't know.

### Paulie Bucco
Twenty-nine. The Zonk nephew — the one everyone pretends doesn't exist. Still 
does occasional work: collections, light intimidation, errands that don't require 
much judgment. The role has been quietly engineered to minimize damage. Nobody 
told him that. He thinks he's just between bigger things.

Functional but visibly sliding. He smokes Zonk the way some people have a drink 
after work and would tell you that if you asked, because it doesn't occur to him 
that it's not a normal thing to say. The addictive personality was always there — 
before Zonk there was drinking, gambling, something else. The family has been 
managing a version of this for years. Zonk is just the version that's harder to 
wave away.

Likeable in the way that people who don't guard themselves are likeable. Talks too 
much, laughs easily, will tell you something he probably shouldn't because it 
didn't occur to him not to. In another life this would be a social gift.

He genuinely loves Richie. Richie genuinely loves him back, which is why nothing 
has been done. Richie sees his brother in Paulie. He can't bring himself to close 
the door.

### Tommy Bucco
Twenty-four. The youngest. Wants to be a comedian. Is doing open mics at Strip 
bars on Tuesday nights in front of twelve people, bombing, and coming home thinking 
it went pretty well.

Does minimal work for the outfit — enough to stay connected, not enough to be 
relied on. Has a notebook he writes bits in. Carries it everywhere. His material 
is observational in a way that's almost there — he notices things, the timing is 
just off. He gets real laughs sometimes, which is enough to keep him going.

He doesn't do material about the family. Not because anyone told him not to — 
nobody has had that conversation because nobody wants to have it. He just hasn't 
gone there yet. Marco watches the notebook and waits.

Richie has never seen him perform. Tommy hasn't invited him. That's probably the 
most honest thing in their relationship.

## Westside Kings Council

Four members. The structural tension is old guard versus new blood, with Cartel 
resentment as the underlying pressure the new blood uses to agitate and the old 
guard tries to manage. The cold war with Dutch splits along the same line — old 
guard maintains it because they remember why it exists, new blood sees it as 
leaving money on the table.

### DeShawn Pruitt
Already documented in main lore. The swing vote. Neither old guard nor new blood — 
the pragmatist both sides think they can bring over, which is why he has more 
actual power than his blocks would suggest. The three hundred dollars has been in 
a ziplock bag in his freezer for four months. He was going to call.

### Curtis "Curt" Waynes
Fifty-eight. Old guard. Southern Westside, the older residential blocks where the 
buildings are bigger and the crews have been the same families for two generations. 
Been on the council since before it was called a council.

Doesn't talk much. When he does, people finish what they're doing first. Not 
because he demands it — he's never demanded anything — but because the last three 
times someone talked over Curt they were wrong and he was right, and that pattern 
accumulates.

His specific memory is a sit-down that went bad in the mid-nineties — a Kings 
lieutenant crossed into the Flats over a debt and Dutch responded in a way that 
cost the Kings four men and two blocks it took six years to recover. Curt was 
twenty-nine. He was the one who had to go to the families. He has not forgotten 
the address of a single one of those houses.

He doesn't hate Dutch. That's what the new blood can't understand about him. He 
just knows exactly what Dutch is capable of and factors it into everything, the 
way you factor weather into a long drive. It's not fear. It's arithmetic.

He knows about Pruitt's ziplock bag and has said nothing because it's not his 
business and also because he finds it quietly hilarious.

### Marcus Webb
Thirty-two. New blood. Runs the northern edge of the Westside where it bleeds into 
the New Build — blocks that didn't exist as Kings territory five years ago. He 
found the gap between the Kings' traditional turf and the Backstreet Boys' 
expansion and moved into it before either side noticed. By the time the council 
was paying attention he was already established.

Articulate in a way that makes council meetings harder than they used to be. Comes 
to every meeting prepared — numbers, comparisons, a version of what the Kings could 
be that sounds almost reasonable until you think about what it would actually 
require. Curt can shut down aggression. He can't as easily shut down a coherent 
argument delivered calmly by someone who has thought about it longer than anyone 
else in the room.

His specific grievance is the Cartel arrangement. He's done the math on what the 
Kings pay upstream annually and it's a number that makes him genuinely angry in a 
focused, productive way. He hasn't done anything about it yet. He's waiting until 
he has the votes.

He and Pruitt have dinner occasionally. Pruitt hasn't told Curt. Marcus considers 
Pruitt the most important person on the council and is patient about it in the way 
that people who are good at waiting are patient — not passive, just willing to let 
time do some of the work.

### Raymond "Ray" Okafor
Forty-four. Central Westside — the dense residential core, the most foot traffic, 
the most corners, the most people. Geographically between Curt's southern territory 
and Marcus's northern edge, which mirrors his council position exactly. He didn't 
plan it that way.

Eight years on the council, second longest after Curt. In that time has never once 
made a speech. Asks questions instead — specific, practical questions about how a 
proposed move would affect specific blocks, specific income, specific relationships. 
The questions are never rhetorical. He actually wants the answers. Both Curt and 
Marcus find this useful and neither has noticed it's also how Ray avoids committing 
to anything until he's ready.

Runs a legitimate car detailing business on Mercer Street that everyone knows about 
and nobody bothers because the tax situation is complicated in ways that benefit 
several parties.

He and Curt respect each other the way two people who have never fully trusted each 
other can respect each other — completely, and from a careful distance. Marcus 
thinks Ray will come around eventually. Ray has been letting Marcus think that for 
two years.

Knows exactly what Pruitt's ziplock bag situation is and has quietly decided it's 
the most human thing about any of them.

## Cahill's IA Problem

The investigation started eight months ago with a spreadsheet that wasn't supposed 
to exist. A civilian records clerk named **Donna Purcell** noticed that a specific 
category of evidence disposals was running on an unusual schedule. She flagged it 
internally to the wrong person, who buried it. Then she flagged it to the right 
person. Donna has since been quietly transferred to a different department. She 
thinks it was routine. It wasn't.

What exists now: a pattern in case closure timing across three precincts, two 
payment anomalies in department procurement that don't trace anywhere useful yet, 
and a name — not Cahill's, a name two steps below him — that keeps appearing at 
the edges of things. Nothing provable. Nothing that would survive a hearing. But 
the shape of it is there if you know what you're looking at.

Cahill doesn't know he's being watched. He runs a careful operation and careful 
operations don't generate obvious signals. He has no reason to look over his 
shoulder, which is the only reason the investigation has gotten this far.

### Detective Sonya Reeves
Thirty-seven. Internal Affairs, eighteen months in the unit. Before that, eight 
years in homicide in a precinct on the east side of Creston that nobody powerful 
cares about, which is probably why she's still clean. Knows how the department 
works from the inside — not from the org chart, from the actual texture of how 
things get done and who benefits.

Not idealistic. She's not doing this because she believes in the system — she's 
doing it because she's good at finding things that don't add up and this particular 
thing doesn't add up in a way she can't leave alone. Idealists make predictable 
moves. Reeves makes useful ones.

Works alone by preference and necessity. Her lieutenant knows she has something. 
He hasn't asked what. That arrangement suits both of them.

Patient in a way that looks like slowness until it doesn't. She's been sitting with 
the same spreadsheet for eight months, adding to it, not acting on it. She won't 
move before it's enough. That discipline is what will eventually make her dangerous.

She doesn't know Cahill's name yet. She will.
