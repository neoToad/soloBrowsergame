# World Lore Core

Canonical setting/faction context extracted from `WORLD_LORE.md`.

## The City

**Creston.** A large fictional composite city. Dense, layered, unglamorous in most places and trying too hard in others. Old infrastructure sits next to new money. Everyone has a grievance. Everyone thinks they're the main character. Characters say "Creston" the way people say their city name when they're not thinking about it — not "this city," just Creston. The name has been worn smooth by use. Nobody remembers who it was named after.

**Tone:** Coen Brothers meets GTA. Dark humor, morally grey, violence that arrives suddenly and then life just continues. Characters who are funny and genuinely dangerous in the same breath.

## Neighborhoods

### The Flats
Old industrial. Warehouses converted or abandoned. Working class holdouts. Businesses that have been there long enough nobody remembers who they actually belong to. Dutch's turf. The streets are wide enough for trucks that stopped coming years ago. The buildings are low enough that you can see the sky, which is the only thing out here that's free.

### The Westside
Dense residential. Corner economy. Generational crews. The Westside Kings' territory. Buildings close together, fire escapes loaded with plants and satellite dishes. Everyone knows everyone. Strangers get noticed.

### The New Build
Gentrifying area. New money moving in, old operators getting squeezed. Flash over substance. Backstreet Boys territory. Glass-front coffee shops next to barbershops that have been there forty years. The old neighborhood is still underneath — you can see it if you know what to look for.

### The Strip
Commercial corridor. Bars, restaurants, high foot traffic. Visible crime dressed up nicely. Shared with the Backstreet Boys. The kind of place where everyone has somewhere to be and nobody asks questions, which is why it works.

### The Hill
Old money adjacent. Legitimate fronts. Where the Bucco Family keeps its respectable face. Quieter than everywhere else. The streets are cleaner. Parking is somehow always available. This is either reassuring or unsettling depending on how long you've been in the city.

### The Docks
Waterfront. Shipping. Old union money. Smuggling infrastructure. Bucco Family territory. Smells like salt and diesel and old fish. The workers who are left treat it like a church. The workers who aren't left are the reason the Buccos do so well.

## Factions

### The Harlan Street Crew
**Turf:** The Flats
**Leader:** Dutch
**Style:** Old school, disciplined, fingers in everything quietly. Been around long enough that their presence feels like the natural order of things. Dutch dabbles in everything — dealing, protection, stolen goods, gambling — but none of it is sloppy. Sends muscle, not lawyers. Works *with* The Blues rather than getting taxed by them.
**Relationships:**
- Cold war with The Westside Kings — mutual respect, mutual distrust, nobody crosses the line officially
- The Blues: allied, not taxed
- Bucco Family: mutual respect, probably the closest thing Dutch has to a peer

**Key figures:**
- **Dutch** — never seen directly in the early game. Known through Morris, through the jobs he sends, through the fact that people lower their voices when his name comes up. Runs the Flats like a property manager: attentive, unsentimental, allergic to waste.
- **Morris** — Dutch's logistics man. Thin, mid-fifties, wears the same beige jacket every time you see him. Calls when there's a job. Hangs up when he's done. Has never once asked how you're doing and gives every indication that it has never occurred to him to wonder.

---

### The Westside Kings
**Turf:** The Westside
**Leadership:** Loose council of four — no one throat to grab, constantly simmering internal tension. The structural fault line is old guard versus new blood, with Cartel resentment as the underlying pressure the new blood uses to agitate and the old guard tries to manage. The cold war with Dutch splits along the same line.
**Income:** Dealing, robbery, prostitution
**Style:** Fast moving, fractious. More dangerous than they look on paper because there's no clear head. Players can exploit internal divisions or get caught in the middle of them.
**Relationships:**
- Cold war with The Harlan Street Crew
- Openly clowned on by the Backstreet Boys, which they resent
- Taxed by The Blues

**Key figures:**

- **DeShawn Pruitt** — one of the council's more stable voices. Runs the northern Westside blocks. Former high school football player with a scholarship that fell through in a way he doesn't like to discuss. Still keeps a football in his apartment. The three hundred dollars he owes you has been in a ziplock bag in his freezer for four months. He was going to call. Neither old guard nor new blood — the pragmatist both sides think they can bring over, which is why he has more actual power than his blocks would suggest.

- **Curtis "Curt" Waynes** — fifty-eight. Old guard. Southern Westside, the older residential blocks where the buildings are bigger and the crews have been the same families for two generations. Been on the council since before it was called a council. Doesn't talk much. When he does, people finish what they're doing first. Not because he demands it — he's never demanded anything — but because the last three times someone talked over Curt they were wrong and he was right, and that pattern accumulates. His specific memory is a sit-down that went bad in the mid-nineties — a Kings lieutenant crossed into the Flats over a debt and Dutch responded in a way that cost the Kings four men and two blocks it took six years to recover. Curt was twenty-nine. He was the one who had to go to the families. He has not forgotten the address of a single one of those houses. He doesn't hate Dutch. That's what the new blood can't understand about him. He just knows exactly what Dutch is capable of and factors it into everything, the way you factor weather into a long drive. It's not fear. It's arithmetic. He knows about Pruitt's ziplock bag and has said nothing because it's not his business and also because he finds it quietly hilarious.

- **Marcus Webb** — thirty-two. New blood. Runs the northern edge of the Westside where it bleeds into the New Build — blocks that didn't exist as Kings territory five years ago. He found the gap between the Kings' traditional turf and the Backstreet Boys' expansion and moved into it before either side noticed. Articulate in a way that makes council meetings harder than they used to be. Comes to every meeting prepared — numbers, comparisons, a version of what the Kings could be that sounds almost reasonable until you think about what it would actually require. His specific grievance is the Cartel arrangement. He's done the math on what the Kings pay upstream annually and it's a number that makes him genuinely angry in a focused, productive way. He hasn't done anything about it yet. He's waiting until he has the votes. He and Pruitt have dinner occasionally. Pruitt hasn't told Curt. Marcus considers Pruitt the most important person on the council and is patient about it in the way that people who are good at waiting are patient — not passive, just willing to let time do some of the work.

- **Raymond "Ray" Okafor** — forty-four. Central Westside — the dense residential core, the most foot traffic, the most corners, the most people. Geographically between Curt's southern territory and Marcus's northern edge, which mirrors his council position exactly. He didn't plan it that way. Eight years on the council, second longest after Curt. In that time has never once made a speech. Asks questions instead — specific, practical questions about how a proposed move would affect specific blocks, specific income, specific relationships. The questions are never rhetorical. He actually wants the answers. Both Curt and Marcus find this useful and neither has noticed it's also how Ray avoids committing to anything until he's ready. Runs a legitimate car detailing business on Mercer Street that everyone knows about and nobody bothers because the tax situation is complicated in ways that benefit several parties. He and Curt respect each other the way two people who have never fully trusted each other can respect each other — completely, and from a careful distance. Marcus thinks Ray will come around eventually. Ray has been letting Marcus think that for two years. Knows exactly what Pruitt's ziplock bag situation is and has quietly decided it's the most human thing about any of them.

- **Terrell** — low-level enforcer. Young, twenty-five, gold chain, clean sneakers. Operates in the gap between what the council sanctions and what nobody's told him he can't do. Smart enough to be dangerous, not smart enough to be careful.

### The Backstreet Boys
**Turf:** The New Build, The Strip
**Leader:** Craig Richards — insists on being called King. Everyone calls him Craig. He hates it.
**Style:** Newer crew. Full of lames. Confuse flash for power. Making decent money but nobody respects them for it. Craig believes every word of his own hype, and his crew believes it too, which makes them unpredictable.
**Relationships:**
- Kings openly clown on them
- Dutch ignores them entirely, which Craig resents deeply
- Taxed by The Blues

**Key figures:**
- **Craig Richards** — mid-thirties, expensive clothes, always slightly overdressed for wherever he is. Has a prepared answer for every slight. Has been preparing them for years. The city's general opinion of Craig is that he'll either be someone eventually or he'll be an example.
- **Darius** — mid-level operator in Craig's orbit, runs Strip-adjacent work. Young, ambitious, and doing it without permission. Misjudged a job recently. Will not say so directly but is accounting for it privately. Formidable. Will be more formidable later, once the current humiliation is processed and filed away.

### The Bucco Family
**Turf:** The Hill, The Docks
**Leader:** Richie Bucco
**Front:** Bucco & Sons Imports — fine olive oil importer. The olive oil is legitimately good.
**Style:** Oldest outfit in Creston. Old world structure. Everyone else in the city is an annoyance rather than a threat. Richie is a mix of Fat Tony's operational laziness and Tony Soprano's genuine menace — will talk to you for forty minutes about cold pressing olive oil and then have something terrible happen before dinner. Family drama bleeds into business drama constantly.
**Relationships:**
- Taxed by The Blues (complicated history)
- Dutch: mutual respect, probably the most functional peer relationship in the city
- Westside Kings and Backstreet Boys: beneath his notice

**Key figures:**

- **Richie Bucco** — sixty-one. Looks like a retired contractor. Wears reading glasses on a beaded cord around his neck. Will actually tell you about olive oil for forty minutes. The menace is real and it arrives without warning. Holds the Cartel connection — he brokered it years ago and has maintained it since. He is not The Cartel's man in Creston; he is Creston's man with The Cartel. The distinction matters. If Richie goes, the connection does not automatically transfer. Every faction in Creston understands this, even if nobody says it out loud. Dutch treats Richie as a peer not out of old-world courtesy but because he understands the arithmetic.

- **Marco Bucco** — thirty-six. The capable nephew. Runs the Docks operation day to day — union relationships, shipping schedules, the parts of the business that require someone who shows up and doesn't make mistakes. Richie trusts him with the infrastructure because Marco treats it like infrastructure: no sentiment, no shortcuts, no drama. What capability costs him is range. He solved the question of what he's willing to do a long time ago and the answer was everything, which means he doesn't have much of an inner life left outside the work. He's not cruel but he's not warm. He has a girlfriend he sees on weekends who knows not to ask questions. He goes to the same restaurant on Fridays. He manages the other two nephews the way he manages a leaking pipe — contain it, don't let it affect the operation, fix it quietly before Richie notices. He is running out of patience for Paulie specifically, which is new. He doesn't dislike Tommy. He just genuinely cannot locate the logic.

- **Paulie Bucco** — twenty-nine. The Zonk nephew — the one everyone pretends doesn't exist. Still does occasional work: collections, light intimidation, errands that don't require much judgment. The role has been quietly engineered to minimize damage. Nobody told him that. He thinks he's just between bigger things. Functional but visibly sliding. Smokes Zonk the way some people have a drink after work and would tell you that if you asked, because it doesn't occur to him that it's not a normal thing to say. The addictive personality was always there — before Zonk there was drinking, gambling, something else. Likeable in the way that people who don't guard themselves are likeable. Talks too much, laughs easily, will tell you something he probably shouldn't because it didn't occur to him not to. He genuinely loves Richie. Richie genuinely loves him back, which is why nothing has been done. Richie sees his brother in Paulie. He can't bring himself to close the door.

- **Tommy Bucco** — twenty-four. The youngest nephew. Wants to be a comedian. Is doing open mics at Strip bars on Tuesday nights in front of twelve people, bombing, and coming home thinking it went pretty well. Does minimal work for the outfit — enough to stay connected, not enough to be relied on. Has a notebook he writes bits in. Carries it everywhere. His material is observational in a way that's almost there — he notices things, the timing is just off. He gets real laughs sometimes, which is enough to keep him going. He doesn't do material about the family. Not because anyone told him not to — nobody has had that conversation because nobody wants to have it. He just hasn't gone there yet. Marco watches the notebook and waits. Richie has never seen him perform. Tommy hasn't invited him. That's probably the most honest thing in their relationship.

### The Blues (Major Crimes Unit)
**Turf:** Citywide
**Leader:** Lieutenant Frank Cahill
**Style:** Fully structured, operates above the other factions. Taxes everyone — except Dutch, with whom Cahill has a relationship built entirely on implication. Bureaucratically polite at all times. Never says anything incriminating. Built this operation himself over decades, which means he understands it from the inside better than anyone.
**What they want:** Protection from internal affairs. Political cover. The money is almost secondary.

**Key figures:**
- **Frank Cahill** — Quiet. Self-made. Goes by Frank. Has gone by Frank for thirty years. You still won't know his first thought about anything. The scariest person in Creston because he has the system on his side and he built his operation from within it. Does not know he is currently under investigation.
- **Detective Sonya Reeves** — thirty-seven. Internal Affairs, eighteen months in the unit. Before that, eight years in homicide in a precinct on the east side of Creston that nobody powerful cares about, which is probably why she's still clean. Not idealistic — she's doing this because she's good at finding things that don't add up and this particular thing doesn't add up in a way she can't leave alone. Works alone by preference and necessity. Patient in a way that looks like slowness until it doesn't. She's been sitting with the same spreadsheet for eight months, adding to it, not acting on it. She won't move before it's enough. She doesn't know Cahill's name yet. She will.

**The IA investigation:** Started eight months ago with a spreadsheet that wasn't supposed to exist. A civilian records clerk named Donna Purcell noticed that a specific category of evidence disposals was running on an unusual schedule. She flagged it internally to the wrong person, who buried it. Then she flagged it to the right person. Donna has since been quietly transferred to a different department. She thinks it was routine. What exists now: a pattern in case closure timing across three precincts, two payment anomalies in department procurement that don't trace anywhere useful yet, and a name — not Cahill's, a name two steps below him — that keeps appearing at the edges of things. Nothing provable. Nothing that would survive a hearing. But the shape of it is there if you know what you're looking at.

## Zonk

The city's drug of choice. Stigmatized publicly, used across all classes. The hypocrisy is citywide and completely unacknowledged.

**Supply chain:** Raw supply comes from The Cartel — no other name is used, because nobody dealing with them locally knows more than that, and the people who do know more don't talk about it. Richie Bucco holds the connection. He brokered the relationship years ago and has maintained it since. He is not The Cartel's man in Creston — he is Creston's man with The Cartel. If Richie goes, the connection does not automatically transfer. The Kings and the Backstreet Boys buy downstream through intermediaries, paying a margin they cannot negotiate away. Craig Richards finds this infuriating. Multiple local cooks cut, process, and distribute independently. Variable quality, territorial disputes over supply, nobody has complete control.

**Lena** — Richie's contact inside The Cartel. No last name used. They meet twice a year, always at Richie's instigation, always on neutral ground — a hotel restaurant in a neighborhood that isn't anybody's. Purely transactional. Not unfriendly, just not interested in anything that isn't the business. She probably knows more about Creston than she lets on. It's not her job to act on it, so she doesn't.

**Methods and effects:**
- **Smoked** — most common, mellow and dissociative, what most people mean when they say they're on Zonk
- **Snorted** — faster, more intense, upper effect, the choice of people who need to function while using
- **Swallowed (pill form)** — slower release, longer high, hallucinogenic edge, harder to dose correctly
- **Injected** — rare, stigmatized even among Zonk users, unpredictable and dangerous, associated with the most desperate users

**Cultural texture:** Everyone uses it. Nobody admits it. A city councilor votes against drug enforcement while her driver picks up her supply. Richie Bucco absolutely does not touch it. His nephew Paulie absolutely does.

## The Cold War

The most dangerous thing in Creston isn't any one faction. It's whatever breaks the cold war between The Harlan Street Crew and The Westside Kings. Both sides know where the line is. Nobody crosses it officially. But there are always people willing to make unofficial moves — and that's where the player lives.

The cold war isn't static. The player will, over the course of the game, make choices that either preserve the balance, exploit the tension, or become the thing that finally tips it. The city doesn't tell you that's what's happening. You figure it out when it's already done.
