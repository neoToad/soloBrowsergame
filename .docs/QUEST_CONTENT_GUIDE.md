# Quest Content & Voice Guide

This document defines the writing standard for all quest content: tone, sentence rhythm, character voice, dialogue, arrival flavors, endings, and scene length. It is the authoritative source for craft decisions.

Structural rules — models, scene types, routing, requirements, flags, YAML formatting — live in `QUEST_AUTHORING_COMPLETE.md`. Read both before authoring a quest. Both are equally binding.

---

## The Tone

Coen Brothers meets GTA. The world is genuinely dangerous and the people in it
are frequently ridiculous — often at the same time. Nobody is joking. Things are
just also funny.

The comedy is never the narrator winking at the player. It lives in:
- Characters who are completely sincere about things that are absurd
- Situations that have their own internal logic, which happens to be insane
- Details that are specific enough to be funny without trying to be
- The gap between how seriously someone takes themselves and how they actually are

The danger is never undercut. Darius walking away doesn't become a gag. But the
guy who runs Dutch's front operation out of a lockup and won't say a word to you
— he can be funny. Pruitt keeping three hundred dollars in a ziplock bag because
he's been planning to call for months and keeps losing his nerve — that's already
funny. You don't need to point at it.

## Sentence Structure

### The core problem to avoid

Short declarative sentences stacked in sequence produce a telegram rhythm that
flattens everything into the same emotional register.

**Wrong:**
> Morris calls at seven in the morning. He has a job. Package pickup on Crane
> Street. Deliver it across the Flats. Three hundred when it's done.

**Right:**
> Morris calls at seven in the morning, which already tells you something — Morris
> is a noon-at-the-earliest kind of person, and a seven a.m. call means either
> the job is time-sensitive or something went wrong upstream that you're about to
> become part of.

The second version takes longer but earns it. The character of Morris is in the
prose, not just the plot.

### The rule

Sentences should connect to each other. Each one should do something the previous
one set up, or push the reader into the next one. Read your scene aloud. If it
sounds like a list, rewrite it until it sounds like a person talking.

Short sentences are a tool, not a rhythm. Use them for impact — a single beat
after a longer run-up — not as the default.

## Character Voice

Every character who speaks or is described should feel like a specific person,
not a type.

### Morris

Transactional. Precise. Slightly disappointing to talk to, in the way that
efficient people are. He calls when he has information and hangs up when he's
done. He uses words like "acceptable" and "noted." He has no evident inner life
but occasionally says something that suggests he might. He probably eats lunch
at the same time every day. Nobody has ever asked him where.

**Wrong (too generic):**
> Morris sounds pleased. He says good work.

**Right:**
> Morris says acceptable, which is, from Morris, something close to a standing
> ovation.

### Dutch

Never on screen in the intro arc. Referenced only through his effects on other
people — what Morris says Dutch wants, what the job implies about how Dutch thinks.
He is to be taken seriously. He is also a man who runs an operation out of a bar
and sends messages through a guy named Morris. Both things are true.

### Sal

Tired. Not defeated — tired. There's a difference. He's been running a dead
friend's bar for four years and it's not working and he's going to keep doing it
anyway, because what else is there. He's funny in the way that stubborn people are
funny — the specificity of his grievance, the dignity he maintains while someone
is telling him his bar is not, in fact, his bar.

### Darius

Young, ambitious, operating without permission. He misjudged a job and he knows it
and he's not going to pretend otherwise, because pretending would be beneath him.
He has a version of himself in his head that is doing very well. That version of
himself did not end up on a pavement outside a Flats walkup revising his estimates.
He's funny because he's so committed to being formidable. He is also, genuinely,
someone to worry about later.

### Vickie

A chaos agent. Does not consider herself to be a chaos agent. Has a completely
coherent internal logic that simply does not account for consequences in the way
that most people's internal logic does. Calls only when she needs something. Loves
the player in her way, which is real but also will immediately generate the next
problem the moment the current one is solved. The comedy of Vickie is that she is
always completely confident.

## Comedy Guidelines

### What works

**Specificity.** The detail that is too specific to be invented. Pruitt's ziplock
bag. The photo behind Sal's register of him and Dennis both grinning, the bar
looking exactly the same in the background as it does now. Morris drinking water
at noon and not touching it. These details are funny because they feel true.

**Commitment.** Characters who take absurd situations completely seriously. A man
who insists on being called King and goes by Craig. A city councilor who has
reframed her Zonk use as a medical necessity and genuinely does not consider
herself a user. Nobody in this city is joking. That's what makes it funny.

**Understatement.** The narration that says less than it means. "Acceptable" from
Morris meaning he's delighted. A victory ending note that says *Dutch knows your
name now* and leaves the player to decide if that's good. A defeat ending that
ends on *you owe Dutch a good job* without elaborating on what happens if you
don't.

**The detail that does double duty.** The soccer ball in Pruitt's apartment. It
makes you feel something and it's also just a little bit funny that it's there,
because of course it is. The best details in this game do both things at once.

**Bathetic juxtaposition.** The serious thing next to the small thing. Dutch is
a name that carries weight in the Flats. Dutch's operation runs through a bar
where two old men have been nursing pints since before you were born. Both things.

### What doesn't work

**The narrator winking.** If the prose is trying to be funny, it isn't. The
comedy lives in the gap between what the narration says and what the player
understands. The moment the narration acknowledges the joke, the joke dies.

**Undermining stakes.** The fight with Darius is not funny. Sal's position is not
funny. The comedy of Vickie does not make her calls for help less real. The tone
can hold danger and absurdity simultaneously but only if the danger is always
genuine. The moment stakes become a punchline the player stops caring.

**Explaining the joke.** If you've written a funny detail and then written a
sentence that explains why it's funny, delete the explanation. The detail was
doing the work. You just undid it.

**Forced banter.** Characters in this city do not quip. They are not witty. They
say exactly what they mean, or they say nothing, and the comedy comes from the
situation around them, not from anything they're consciously doing.

## What the Prose Must Not Do

### State its own themes

The scene ends. The player feels something. That's the whole job. If the closing
line is explaining what the player should take away, cut it.

**Wrong:**
> You told the truth and got paid for it. In this city that's not nothing.

**Right:**
> You told the truth and got paid for it.

The second line is already the whole observation. The city comment is the writer
losing their nerve.

### Summarise what just happened

Arrival flavors and closing lines are not recaps. They are the last beat. They
should land on something new — an image, a small fact, a tone — not fold back
over the scene.

**Wrong:**
> You walk home thinking about the package and what was in it and whether you
> made the right call.

**Right:**
> The split knuckles take three days to close.

The second line implies everything the first one says and costs four words.

### Use adverbs to cover for verbs

If you need an adverb, find a better verb. "Walks slowly" is "shuffles" or
"drags" or "takes his time." "Says quietly" is usually just "says" — the
quietness should be in the context, not the modifier.

### Name emotions directly

Show the condition instead. Not: *he's scared.* Show the chain on the door, the
rehearsed line delivered like he doesn't believe it will work, the way he keeps
looking at the exit.

## Dialogue

Direct dialogue is encouraged. Characters should speak in their own voice, in
quotation marks, when the line is specific enough to earn it. A character's way
of talking is part of who they are — Morris's clipped precision, Darius's
deliberate calm, Vickie's breezy confidence that everything will work out — and
direct dialogue is often the fastest way to put that on the page.

### What good dialogue does

It sounds like a specific person and nobody else. If you could swap the line into
another character's mouth without changing it, rewrite it.

It carries subtext. What the character says and what they mean are not always the
same thing, and the gap between them is where the scene lives.

It doesn't over-explain. Characters in this city say what they need to say and
stop. Nobody delivers speeches. Nobody summarises their own feelings.

### Rhythm and attribution

Keep attribution simple. "He says" is almost always enough. Avoid:
- Adverb-loaded attribution: *he says quietly, she says carefully*
- Performative attribution: *he growls, she snaps, he barks*

Let the line itself carry the weight. If the line needs "he says menacingly" to
land as menacing, the line isn't doing its job.

Dialogue and action should breathe together. A line followed immediately by what
the character does — or doesn't do — is almost always more alive than a line
followed by another line.

**Example:**

> "I misjudged the job," Darius says. He's not apologising. He's accounting.
> Then he tells you to pass something on to Dutch — just his name, just hello —
> and he walks. No hurry. Doesn't check if you're watching.

The dialogue is one sentence. Everything else is action and observation. That
ratio is roughly right.

### Choice labels

Choice labels are the one place the player speaks directly. They can and should
use quotation marks when the player is saying something. Keep them punchy —
a choice label is a line reading, not a stage direction.

**As a line:**
> "Tell him you're not here to negotiate"

**As a stage direction (avoid):**
> Walk over and make it clear you're serious

## Arrival Flavors

One line. One image or one dry fact. It lands before the next scene loads and
should feel like a cut, not a summary.

The test: read it in isolation. Does it do something? Does it leave you somewhere
specific? Or is it explaining what just happened?

**Wrong:**
> You walk home thinking about what you did and whether it was right.

**Wrong:**
> The city keeps score differently than you do.

**Right:**
> The walk back is longer than the walk there. You're not sure why.

**Right:**
> Morris says acceptable. You take it.

**Right:**
> Third floor. The door opens before you knock.

## Endings

Ending scenes carry the most weight and are the most commonly overwritten. The
instinct is to give the player a conclusion. Resist it. The game continues. The
city continues. The ending scene is not a verdict — it's a last image before the
hub loads.

**What ending scenes should do:**
- Give the player one concrete thing to hold onto (the cash on the counter, the
  unanswered text, the empty corkboard)
- Acknowledge the emotional reality without naming it
- Set up something unresolved that the next quest can find

**What ending scenes should not do:**
- Resolve the moral question the quest raised
- Tell the player how to feel about what they did
- End with a maxim or a thesis

## Scene Length

**Entrance scenes:** Long enough to establish the situation fully. The player
needs to understand what they're walking into and why it matters. Two to four
paragraphs.

**Normal scenes:** One to three paragraphs. Enough to establish the beat and let
the choice land in context.

**Roll setup scenes:** Brief. Two or three sentences. The situation is already
established. The roll scene narrows focus to the moment of action.

**Ending scenes:** Two to four paragraphs. Enough to land. Not enough to explain.

**Arrival flavors:** One sentence. Always.

## The Checklist

Before submitting any scene body text, read it against these:

- [ ] Does it read aloud like a person, or like a list?
- [ ] Is there at least one specific detail that feels true?
- [ ] Is there anything funny in it — not trying to be funny, just true in a way
      that happens to be funny?
- [ ] Does it state its own theme anywhere? (Cut that sentence.)
- [ ] Does any dialogue sound like it could belong to a different character? (Make it specific.)
- [ ] Does the closing line summarise what happened? (Find a last image instead.)
- [ ] Are there adverbs covering for weak verbs? (Find the verb.)
- [ ] Is any emotion named directly? (Show the condition instead.)
